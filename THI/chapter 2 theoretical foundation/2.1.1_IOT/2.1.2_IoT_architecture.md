### 2.1.2 Core Architecture and Components

Understanding how IoT systems work requires examining their architecture—the way different components fit together to enable the capabilities discussed earlier. While IoT implementations vary widely depending on application, most follow a similar layered structure where each layer performs specific functions and communicates with adjacent layers (Al-Fuqaha et al., 2015). This modular approach offers practical advantages: organizations can start with basic implementations and expand gradually, replace individual components without redesigning entire systems, and select technologies appropriate to their specific needs and constraints.

IoT architectures typically organize into distinct layers that move data from the physical world through progressive stages of abstraction and intelligence. Although architectural models vary slightly across studies—some proposing three layers, others four or five—the fundamental principle remains consistent: systematic separation of concerns between sensing, communication, processing and application delivery (Sethi and Sarangi, 2017). Most implementations can be understood through three principal layers:

- the **Perception Layer**: capturing physical data. 
- the **Network Layer**: handling data transmission.
- the **Application Layer**: delivering services to users.

Each layer addresses specific technical challenges while interacting closely with adjacent layers to ensure continuous data flow.

#### **Perception Layer: Sensing and Actuation**

The perception layer forms the physical interface between digital systems and the real world. Often called the device layer or sensory layer, this is where sensors measure parameters like soil moisture, temperature, humidity, light intensity or nutrient concentrations, converting physical phenomena into digital signals. Equally important are actuators—devices that execute physical actions such as opening irrigation valves, activating pumps or adjusting environmental controls based on system decisions.

##### **Perception Network**

The perception network supports communication between perception nodes and higher architectural layers. Its primary responsibility is transmitting data collected by sensors to gateway devices or communication nodes, which forward this data to network infrastructure. Conversely, it delivers control commands from higher layers back to actuators within the perception layer (Al-Fuqaha et al., 2015). Communication within the perception network may rely on short-range wireless technologies optimized for low power consumption and local connectivity, including Zigbee, Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE), or proprietary protocols designed for specific applications. Security and reliability are essential considerations at this stage, as data integrity and timely transmission directly influence effectiveness of subsequent processing and decision-making (Sethi and Sarangi, 2017).

#### **Network Layer: Communication Infrastructure**

The network layer, also called the transmission or transport layer, handles data movement between perception devices and processing systems. 

The network layer integrates heterogeneous communication technologies, legacy infrastructure and diverse transmission protocols to ensure reliable data transfer. Modern IoT systems must bridge multiple network types—from short-range personal area networks to long-range cellular or satellite links—requiring sophisticated routing, addressing and protocol translation mechanisms (Gubbi et al., 2013). This layer can be conceptually divided into access networks, core networks, and specialized wide-area networks, each serving distinct connectivity functions.

#### **Application Layer: User Interfaces and Services**

The application layer constitutes the topmost level of IoT architecture and represents the primary point of interaction between the system and end users. Its role is delivering application-specific services based on data collected and processed by lower layers, transforming processed information into visualizations, alerts, reports and control interfaces that support decision-making (Al-Fuqaha et al., 2015).

Applications may be accessed through web interfaces, mobile devices or specialized terminals, and can be tailored to user roles and requirements. This layer often subdivides into the **application support layer** (providing backend processing, data management and service infrastructure) and the **application service layer** delivering user-facing functionality (Sethi and Sarangi, 2017).

##### **Application Support Layer**

The application support layer provides computational and service infrastructure for IoT applications. It handles intelligent data processing, validation, filtering and categorization, including identification of erroneous or irrelevant data. This layer typically relies on cloud computing platforms offering scalable storage and processing capabilities, Service-Oriented Architecture (SOA) principles enabling modular system integration, and middleware facilitating interoperability between heterogeneous devices and applications (Gubbi et al., 2013).

**Cloud computing** has become fundamental to IoT application support, providing essentially unlimited storage for historical data, powerful computing for sophisticated analytics, and accessibility from any internet-connected device. Cloud services handle software updates automatically, provide built-in redundancy and backups, and scale effortlessly as systems grow from pilot projects to full deployments. The subscription model reduces upfront investment though it creates ongoing operational costs. Major cloud platforms (AWS IoT, Microsoft Azure IoT, Google Cloud IoT) offer specialized services designed specifically for IoT workloads, including device management, data ingestion pipelines and pre-built analytical tools (Gubbi et al., 2013; Al-Fuqaha et al., 2015).

**Machine-to-Machine (M2M) communication** models enable devices to exchange information directly, supporting automation and real-time coordination without human intervention. M2M principles underpin many IoT application support functions, allowing sensors to trigger actuators automatically, enabling peer-to-peer device collaboration, and facilitating distributed decision-making (Sethi and Sarangi, 2017).

Data organization becomes critical at this layer. IoT systems generate continuous streams of measurements requiring efficient storage and rapid retrieval. Time-series databases specialized for this purpose make it practical to query historical patterns, compare current conditions against past performance, and identify long-term trends. Without proper data management, systems drown in numbers without gaining insight (Al-Fuqaha et al., 2015).

##### **Application Service Layer and User Interaction**

The application service layer delivers user-facing functionality—dashboards displaying current conditions, mobile apps sending alerts, control interfaces for manual intervention, and analytical tools revealing patterns in historical data. Despite being the top layer architecturally, this layer often determines whether users adopt and trust the system (Tzounis et al., 2017).

Good interface design balances comprehensiveness with simplicity. Dashboards displaying every available metric overwhelm users; dashboards that oversimplify hide important nuances. Effective designs prioritize information users need for decisions—current status, alerts requiring attention, and trends indicating developing situations—while making detailed data accessible when needed.

The application layer also encompasses integration with external services. Modern agricultural IoT systems rarely operate in isolation. They may consume weather forecasts to adjust irrigation schedules, integrate with management software tracking inputs and yields, or connect to market information systems. These integrations multiply system value but require standardized data formats and robust Application Programming Interfaces (APIs) (Tzounis et al., 2017).

However, integration across layers presents challenges. Components from different vendors must communicate through standardized protocols and data formats. Compatibility issues can emerge unexpectedly, requiring technical expertise to resolve.
