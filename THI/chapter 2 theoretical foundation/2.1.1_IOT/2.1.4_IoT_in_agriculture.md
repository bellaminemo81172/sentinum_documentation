2.1.4 IoT in Agriculture: An Emerging Application Domain
According to Tzounis et al. (2017), the growing adoption of Internet of Things (IoT) technologies reflects a fundamental shift in how agricultural systems are designed and managed. Modern agriculture faces persistent pressures, including increasing water scarcity, climate variability, labor shortages, and rising food demand driven by global population growth. In response to these constraints, farming practices are progressively evolving toward approaches commonly described as smart agriculture or precision agriculture, which emphasize the use of digital technologies to support more efficient and adaptive decision‑making.
Within this context, IoT enables agricultural systems to move beyond episodic field observations toward continuous and structured information flows. By deploying connected sensors and devices across farms, data related to soil conditions, crop status, and local climate variability can be collected and transferred through communication networks for analysis. This data‑driven paradigm contrasts with traditional farming practices, which largely relied on experience and periodic inspection, and supports more responsive management of agricultural inputs. As a result, IoT contributes to agricultural systems that are better able to adapt to environmental variability and resource constraints.
Key Application Areas


Precision Farming and Crop Management
IoT technologies support site‑specific crop management by enabling informed adjustment of agricultural inputs based on actual field conditions. Soil sensors, for example, provide real‑time information on moisture availability, allowing irrigation to be applied only when and where needed. Similarly, variable‑rate application systems use sensor data to optimize fertilizer and pesticide use, reducing unnecessary input application while maintaining crop performance. Studies report that IoT‑enabled precision irrigation systems can reduce water consumption by 20–40% without compromising yields (Ayaz et al., 2019).


Smart and Automated Irrigation Systems
Among agricultural IoT applications, irrigation management is one of the most mature and widely implemented. Smart irrigation systems integrate soil moisture measurements, weather information, and crop water requirement models to determine optimal irrigation schedules. Automated valves and pumps respond dynamically to measured conditions rather than fixed time‑based routines. This approach is especially valuable in water‑scarce regions, where inefficient water use directly threatens both production viability and environmental sustainability.


Environmental Monitoring and Decision Support
IoT sensor networks enable continuous observation of environmental conditions such as temperature, humidity, and atmospheric variability. When combined with data analytics tools, these observations support forecasting of disease pressure, pest activity, and suitable periods for planting or harvesting.


Livestock Monitoring and Management
IoT applications also extend to animal husbandry through wearable or embedded sensors that monitor livestock health and behavior. Parameters such as location, movement, and body temperature can indicate early signs of illness or stress, allowing timely intervention. These systems also support optimized feeding strategies and pasture utilization, contributing to improved animal welfare and production efficiency (Ayaz et al., 2019).


Automation and Remote Farm Operations
IoT‑enabled agricultural machinery—including tractors, harvesters, and aerial platforms—supports increased automation and remote operation. GPS‑guided equipment allows precise execution of tasks such as planting and harvesting, while connected monitoring systems enable farmers to supervise operations remotely. Through mobile or web‑based interfaces, farm activities can be monitored and adjusted in real time, increasing operational flexibility and reducing labor dependency (Choudhary et al., 2025).


IoT as a Socio‑Technical Transformation and Sustainability Enabler in Agriculture
The adoption of IoT technologies in agriculture represents more than a technological upgrade; it reflects a broader socio‑technical transformation in how farming decisions are made, evaluated, and implemented. In traditional agricultural systems, farmers relied primarily on personal experience, routine practices, and periodic field observations to guide management decisions. In smart agriculture, IoT systems introduce continuous streams of information that support and sometimes automate these decisions, shifting the farmer's role from sole decision‑maker to decision supervisor. While human knowledge and agronomic expertise remain central, decisions are increasingly informed by data generated from interconnected systems, improving consistency, transparency, and accountability in farm management.

Beyond decision support, IoT has become a key instrument for measuring, monitoring, and demonstrating sustainability in agriculture. Smart farming systems make it possible to quantify water consumption, energy use, input efficiency, and environmental conditions in ways that were previously impractical. This capability is particularly relevant in contexts where agriculture must respond to environmental constraints, regulatory requirements, or sustainability objectives. By providing traceable and measurable indicators, IoT enables farmers not only to improve resource efficiency but also to document these improvements over time.

The role of IoT as a sustainability enabler is especially evident in controlled and emerging farming systems. In greenhouse agriculture, IoT platforms regulate and document variables such as temperature, humidity, CO₂ concentration, and light intensity, allowing producers to optimize growing conditions while minimizing energy and water consumption. Similarly, vertical farming systems rely almost entirely on IoT‑driven monitoring and automation to manage lighting cycles, nutrient delivery, and climate control within highly constrained spaces. In these environments, smart farming is inseparable from digital control, as productivity and sustainability depend on precise coordination of system variables.

IoT also plays an increasing role in urban agriculture, where space limitations, proximity to consumers, and environmental performance are critical factors. Rooftop farms, indoor cultivation facilities, and community‑based urban farms integrate IoT systems to manage limited resources efficiently and to ensure consistent production under non‑traditional growing conditions. In such cases, smart farming technologies support localized food production while contributing to broader sustainability goals related to reduced transportation, optimized land use, and transparent resource management.

Across these diverse agricultural contexts, IoT reinforces the transition toward farming systems that are not only more productive, but also more measurable, verifiable, and adaptable. Sustainability is no longer assessed solely through outcomes such as yield, but through continuous monitoring of how resources are used and environmental conditions are managed. In this sense, IoT enables agriculture to move from implicit sustainability practices to explicit, data‑supported sustainability strategies.


Market Growth and Adoption Trends
The growing integration of IoT in agriculture is reflected in sustained market expansion and increasing adoption across different farming systems. The global smart agriculture market expanded from approximately USD 13.8 billion in 2020 to around USD 20.9 billion by 2025, corresponding to an annual growth rate close to 10% (MarketsandMarkets, 2020). This growth indicates that IoT‑based agricultural solutions are progressing beyond pilot projects and research trials toward broader commercial deployment.

Adoption patterns further demonstrate this transition. While IoT technologies were initially adopted mainly by large or technologically advanced farms, they are increasingly being implemented across a wider range of agricultural operations. Global adoption rates rose from 26% of agricultural enterprises in 2021 to approximately 58% by 2024, with forecasts suggesting that adoption may exceed 70% within the next few years (IoT Analytics, 2021; AgFunder, 2024). These trends are supported by decreasing sensor costs, improved system reliability, and longer device lifespans, which reduce both technical and economic barriers.

Economic viability has become a decisive factor in accelerating adoption. Farm‑level analyses indicate that investments in smart irrigation and related IoT systems often achieve return‑on‑investment periods between two and four years, driven by reductions in water consumption, labor demands, and input costs, as well as by more stable production outcomes (Tzounis et al., 2017). As a result, IoT is increasingly perceived not as an experimental technology, but as a practical component of modern agricultural management and long‑term sustainability planning.
