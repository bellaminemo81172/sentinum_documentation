## 2 THEORETICAL FOUNDATION

### 2.1 Olive Tree Ecophysiology and Water Use

Olive trees (*Olea europaea* L.) are one of the most important crops in Mediterranean agriculture. Their importance comes not only from their economic value, but also from their ability to grow under dry summer conditions. Olive trees are well adapted to water-limited environments, yet this does not mean that they need little water in every situation. Rather, they use water more efficiently than many other orchard crops.

This adaptation is linked to both leaf structure and root behavior. Olive leaves are generally small, thick, and protected by a waxy surface. Their internal structure also helps reduce excessive transpiration when atmospheric demand is high (Ennajeh et al., 2010). Below ground, olive trees develop broad root systems that can explore a large soil volume when soil depth and structure are favorable. In well-drained soils, roots can use both upper and deeper horizons, which helps the tree remain productive during dry periods.

For irrigation management, this root behavior is important because water should be evaluated across the active root zone, not only at the soil surface. This is one reason why shallow and infrequent observations are often not enough for reliable irrigation decisions in olive orchards.

Olive drought resistance also depends on physiological regulation. When water becomes scarce, the tree reduces water loss by closing its stomata. It also activates internal adjustment mechanisms that help maintain basic cell function. Research has shown that drought response differs across cultivars, especially in the way roots and vascular tissues manage water transport under stress (Lopez-Bernal et al., 2020). In practical terms, this means that olive trees can tolerate moderate water deficit better than many fruit crops, but this tolerance has clear limits. If water restriction is too strong or occurs at sensitive stages, both yield and oil quality decline.

Another important point is that olive water use is not constant throughout the year. Evapotranspiration changes with canopy size, climatic demand, orchard density, and phenological stage. As a result, irrigation should not be based on a single fixed rule for the whole season. Even for a drought-adapted crop such as olive, good irrigation management requires matching water supply to growth stage, soil conditions, and production objectives.

### 2.2 Water Requirements and Irrigation Management of Olive Trees

The water needs of olive trees change over the season. These changes are closely linked to phenological development. Water stress during flowering and fruit set is especially harmful because it can reduce flower viability, fruit retention, and final yield (Fereres and Soriano, 2007). Water demand remains important during fruit enlargement, while water supply during oil accumulation is more closely related to oil quantity and composition. For this reason, irrigation should be treated as a stage-dependent management tool rather than as a uniform seasonal input.

Crop water requirements are commonly estimated through the relationship $ET_c = ET_0 \times K_c$. In this equation, $ET_0$ represents reference evapotranspiration and $K_c$ is the crop coefficient. For olive orchards, typical $K_c$ values usually range from about 0.40 to 0.70 depending on canopy development, orchard density, irrigation method, and local climate (Orgaz et al., 2006; Pastor et al., 2021). Lower values are usually observed during early vegetative stages, while higher values occur during active canopy and fruit development.

Soil moisture availability is equally important. Irrigation decisions should not rely only on calendar schedules, because olive performance depends on the interaction between weather conditions, soil water-holding capacity, and root-zone depletion. Field capacity defines the upper limit of available water after drainage, while the permanent wilting point marks the lower limit beyond which the tree cannot recover normal function. In practice, irrigation is often triggered before management allowable depletion exceeds roughly 40-50% of available water, especially during sensitive growth stages (Marino et al., 2022). This threshold-based logic is more reliable than fixed irrigation intervals because it responds to real field conditions.

Overall, irrigation management is a balancing process. Too little water during critical stages reduces yield. Too much water wastes resources, increases disease risk, and can lower oil quality. In Mediterranean conditions, the most effective strategies often protect flowering and fruit set while allowing controlled stress during less sensitive periods such as pit hardening. This is why regulated deficit irrigation has become such an important approach in modern olive production.

### 2.3 Effects of Irrigation on Yield and Olive Oil Quality

Irrigation has a direct effect on both fruit production and oil characteristics. When water deficits occur during flowering, fruit set, or early fruit growth, fruit number and fruit size usually decline. This lowers total yield and oil output (Gucci et al., 2019; Iniesta et al., 2009). Severe or prolonged stress may also reduce harvest uniformity and increase seasonal variability. In commercial terms, poor irrigation management does not only reduce biological performance; it also makes production less predictable.

Oil quality responds differently. Moderate and well-timed water restriction can improve the concentration of phenolic compounds and antioxidants. These compounds contribute to bitterness, pungency, oxidative stability, and overall sensory character (Dag et al., 2018; Romero et al., 2020). By contrast, excessive irrigation tends to dilute these quality-related compounds and may reduce the functional and commercial value of the oil. In other words, the irrigation strategy that maximizes fruit biomass is not necessarily the one that produces the best oil. This distinction is especially important in Mediterranean olive systems that compete on quality rather than on volume alone.

This quality-quantity trade-off explains the importance of deficit irrigation strategies in Mediterranean olive cultivation. Regulated Deficit Irrigation (RDI) reduces water supply during selected, less sensitive stages, while Sustained Deficit Irrigation (SDI) applies a more uniform reduction across the season (Caruso et al., 2020). Among these approaches, RDI is often considered more effective because it protects sensitive phases while still improving water productivity and, in many cases, oil quality. The goal is therefore not simply to reduce irrigation, but to reduce it at the right time and to the right degree.

For a smart irrigation concept, the key implication is clear: irrigation targets should not be defined only by maximum water replacement. They should instead reflect the production objective, whether that objective prioritizes total yield, premium oil quality, or a compromise between both.

### 2.4 Irrigation Methods and the Need for Smart Irrigation

Traditional irrigation systems such as basin, furrow, and surface flooding are still used in parts of the Mediterranean, especially where farms are small and investment capacity is limited. These systems are simple and familiar, but their efficiency is generally low. Water distribution is often uneven, and large losses occur through runoff, percolation, and evaporation (Farooq et al., 2021). Over time, such practices may also contribute to soil degradation, waterlogging in some areas, and salt accumulation in others.

Modern irrigation systems, particularly drip irrigation, offer a more suitable basis for olive cultivation under water scarcity. Drip systems apply water locally, improve distribution control, and make it easier to align irrigation with actual plant demand. When combined with sensors, monitoring tools, and automated control, they can also reduce unnecessary water application and support more stable production outcomes (Caruso et al., 2020; Testi et al., 2021).

The difference between traditional and smart irrigation is therefore not only technological but also managerial. Traditional methods tend to follow fixed routines, while smart systems rely on measured field conditions, climatic information, and stage-specific crop needs. This transition is especially important in Mediterranean olive production, where irrigation decisions influence not only yield but also oil quality, water productivity, and long-term soil condition (Intrigliolo et al., 2019). Smart irrigation should therefore be understood as a management approach that improves timing, precision, and responsiveness, rather than as a simple addition of sensors to an existing system.

Another important issue in system choice is hydraulic uniformity. A smart irrigation system may generate good recommendations, but it will not perform well if water is not distributed evenly across the orchard. Pressure variation, emitter spacing, slope, and clogging risk all affect whether each tree receives the intended amount of water. For this reason, irrigation design must consider not only digital control, but also the physical reliability of the hydraulic network. In practice, a technically advanced system with poor distribution uniformity may perform worse than a simpler system with stable and predictable water delivery.

System selection also depends on which variables are monitored and how important they are to control decisions. Some variables are essential because they directly affect irrigation timing and system performance. These include soil moisture, flow rate, pressure, and basic climatic information such as air temperature and rainfall. Other variables, such as soil salinity, relative humidity, and soil temperature, provide additional decision support and become especially relevant in water-scarce or saline environments. More advanced variables, including canopy temperature, satellite imagery, or vegetation indices, may improve decision quality further, but they also increase cost and complexity. This means that the monitoring strategy should be matched to the actual needs and financial capacity of the farm.

The choice of control logic is equally important. At the simplest level, irrigation can be timer-based, where water is applied according to a fixed schedule. A more responsive option is threshold-based control, where irrigation starts when soil moisture falls below a defined limit. More advanced systems use evapotranspiration-based control, which estimates crop water demand from weather conditions, or hybrid control, which combines weather data, soil measurements, and phenological stage. For olive cultivation, hybrid approaches are often the most suitable because they connect agronomic thresholds with real-time field conditions. However, they also require more sensors, more calibration, and more user understanding.

Farm size must also be considered when evaluating irrigation concepts. Small farms often need simple, low-cost systems that are easy to maintain and do not depend heavily on technical support. Medium-sized commercial orchards may benefit from a balance between affordability and automation, especially where water costs are significant. Large or intensive orchards can justify more advanced monitoring and control because the potential gains in water savings, labor efficiency, and yield stability are greater. As a result, there is no single "best" smart irrigation system for all olive farms. The most appropriate solution depends on scale, management capacity, and economic constraints.

Climate change strengthens the need for this transition. Rising temperatures, more frequent drought events, and greater climatic variability are expected to increase irrigation uncertainty and intensify pressure on water resources in olive-growing regions (Chartzoulakis and Psarras, 2017; Santos et al., 2021; Tanasijevic et al., 2019). Under these conditions, irrigation management must become more adaptive, more precise, and more closely linked to real-time field information. This creates a clear rationale for smart irrigation systems in Mediterranean olive cultivation and provides a natural transition toward the following chapter on IoT-based irrigation concepts.
